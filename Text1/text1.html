The Marrow Thieves

Cherie Dimaline

Big Ideas: Family and community, resilience, survival, resistance, power of story, hope, human nature and conscience

I find dystopian novels very appealing. Their endless possibilities, conflicts, and struggles all make them enjoyable to read. Cherie Dimaline's use of family and community in this dystopian society with The Marrow Thieves – where people can no longer dream and are going insane – demonstrates hope and resilience. French’s family consists of numerous members with various traits and purposes, with Miigwans being the most prominent. He is the elder in the group and is responsible for leading and passing on stories. After French kills Travis, who took Riri away from them; he is bothered and lost, but Miigwans reminds him that “sometimes you do things you would not do in another time and place. Sometimes the path in front of you alters. Sometimes it goes through some pretty dark territory. Just make sure it doesn’t change the intent of the trip. As long as the intent is good, nothing else matters.” (Dimaline 145). It can be difficult to do unpleasant things even with good intentions because of troublesome circumstances; however, Miigwans’ wise words demonstrate resilience and the understanding that survival sometimes necessitates difficult choices and remind me that these circumstances are often unavoidable, and it is crucial to possess the correct mindset to move on. This statement also foreshadows the difficult path that French must overcome to survive and inspire hope. Furthermore, family and community are heavily emphasized to demonstrate their importance to survival and resistance. When French’s family gathers for a discussion, the thought that “We were family. We were all we had. The rest was dark and unknown” (54) sparks in unison among them. The metaphorical comparison of “dark” and “unknown” to the outside world effectively highlights the plot’s setting, demonstrating both the family’s conditions and the impact of external influences. After everyone loses their sovereignty over their emotions and actions following the loss of two family members, they continue to survive and remain strong because of family and community; they support and heal one another through words, actions, and existence. Family and community are critical to survival and resistance in such a world because they build resilience within everyone. I understand the danger of loneliness, especially in unpleasant circumstances, and this novel highlights the importance of a reliable family and community during hardships. Finally, during a discussion, Wab asks whether “circumstances make people turn bad? Or that people make circumstances bad to begin with?” (53). In their society, people have turned evil and begun harvesting Indigenous people's marrows for dreams. Some can say that this sinful act is triggered by the circumstances of dreamlessness and apocalyptic society, but some can say it is because of human’s iniquitous nature. The use of juxtaposition in that question demonstrates the controversial theme of human nature and conscience; it made me reflect on the current society’s many evil doings, such as war – whether we are born evil or made evil. Overall, this novel not only reflects on survival, family and community, and resilience but further explores the theme of human nature and conscience. In today’s society, where conflict frequently arises and feelings of hopelessness prevail, it is essential to have a supportive family and community to rely on and a strong foundation of human morals to survive and thrive.
