Cottagers Vs Indians

CBC

Land rights, culture preservation, land use, minority privilege, food sovereignty

Land and treaty rights have been ongoing issues the Indigenous community faces, and despite the government’s numerous policies to improve and address those issues, they are far from resolved. In particular, the documentary Cottagers Vs Indians by CBC highlights the conflict between Indigenous people and society over a lake through the lens of Indigenous and First Nations and the general society. James Whetung, a member of the Curve Lake First Nation, is determined to plant and harvest wild rice in Pigeon Lake and to preserve and restore the culture, hereditary territory, food sovereignty, and the then-destroyed ecosystem of the lake. He hosted workshops and tours surrounding wild rice to provide consultation services to communities and spread the culture through social and community gatherings. The purpose and intent of planting wild rice appear charitable and beneficent.; however, the execution causes conflict and controversy. Indigenous people had a history of lands being taken away from them; therefore, it is logical to preserve and protect their land rights. However, Mr. Whetung decided to plant a large amount of wild rice across the lake, causing major inconvenience for nearby cottagers, residents’ lifestyles, and boat traffic. He refused to stop and intentionally plant them in someone’s areas even after receiving complaints, confrontations, and suggestions, such as “creating designated areas for wild rice harvesting, [so] boat traffic wouldn’t go through there. It wouldn’t affect the rice, it wouldn’t damage the rice, and by the same token, [the residents] would have our areas” (CBC 28:35). Even though Mr. Whetung has a constitution stating his First Nation’s control and ownership over the lake, and his intent is well and benevolence, his act may be unnecessary and selfish from society’s lens. Furthermore, Mr. Whetung planted way more wild rice than needed, and “[they] now have something out here that is a farm; it’s all seeded, and it’s 1800 acres plus" (33:27). However, “He can’t do it all in a year; it’s more than he can possibly harvest. So why continue to expand it?” (33:34). This sparked further controversy about whether Mr. Whetung's action to plant wild rice all across for food sovereignty, culture preservation, and land rights is justified and logical or is just an act of revenge, anger, and egocentric. CBC’s balanced interviews from both sides of this conflict have challenged me to view land and Indigenous rights issues through both societal and Indigenous lenses. It also makes me reflect on the topic of minority privilege—whether Indigenous people, such as Mr. Whetung, are using their history of suffering and iniquity to justify their excessive and potentially self-centred actions. Oftentimes, there are solutions to land rights that can satisfy both parties; instead of fighting for land rights and authority, we should create respected boundaries, treaties, and mutual agreements that benefit both ends. Similarly, in The Marrow Thieves and Hunting By Stars, the government had taken over all Indigenous land and violated their right to peace instead of finding a solution that is mutually beneficial and minimizes conflict and war.
