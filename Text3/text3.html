Hunting By Stars

Cherie Dimaline

Big Ideas: Sacrifice, family, reunion, assimilation, integration, residential schools

As a sequel to The Marrow Thieves, both texts explore similar topics and themes; nevertheless, Hunting By Stars by Cherie Dimaline introduces several new characters, with Mitch being a prominent one. Mitch, French’s brother, who the school captures at the start of The Marrow Thieves, unites with his brother after French gets taken into the school. French constantly thinks and dreams about his brother after the separation, and this reunion may seem to be something worth celebrating. However, Mitch is completely integrated, brainwashed, and assimilated into the school and its beliefs, since "after some time, after being taught the reality of the world and the important role we could play in it, well, [he] started to see the light" (Dimaline 80). He also claims that “it was nobody’s fault but [his] own that [his] leg got broken. If [he] had only listened to instructions when they found us in that tree house, nobody would have gotten hurt” (78). This demonstrates the danger and influence of residential schools over one’s identity and culture; how the school in the text, as well as in Canadian history, eliminates self-identity and unique quality over thousands of Indigenous people and children, leading to culture loss and generational trauma. Furthermore, French makes numerous attempts to awaken Mitch to the truth, but his efforts are in vain and fail to the point where French had to kill his brother with a bow and arrow when “[he] released [his] fingers from the string” (289) during a confrontation. French lost his sovereignty over his brother and himself, resulting in Mitch’s death. This exemplifies the detrimental aspects of residential school, and how it can fundamentally alter a person, reshaping their identity entirely. Despite Mitch having the memory of his past, he simply ignores it, leaving his old self and family behind both literally and figuratively. This is reflective of real-life society, where culture and uniqueness are taken away from Indigenous people, forcing them to leave their old selves and families. Additionally, French’s decision to kill and harm his own family – his brother – may be unethical and wrongful; however, after what Mitch had said and done, it challenges me to think about whether he is French’s brother anymore and whether this act of French is justified or impulsive. In conclusion, this text connects with The Marrow Thieves because they are in the same series and they explore very similar topics, but it also connects with Orange as they both explore the topic of family and their passing and the grief that comes afterwards.
