Orange

Shane Hawk

Big Ideas: Grief, regrets, family, resilience, death, violence

Death, a frightening topic that many would avoid, is inevitable, and so is grief. The protagonist in Orange by Shane Hawk, undergoes immense grief after the deaths of his wife and the sister of someone close.. After “the crash that tossed [the protagonist’s wife] through the windshield when [he] hit that tree. The crash that took her away from [him] for good. Not only her, but Devon's sister” (Hawk 36), the protagonist faces an enormous amount of grief and regrets, and he loses his sovereignty over his emotions. The loss of someone, particularly a family member, can be profoundly demoralizing. When their passing relates to your actions or circumstances, it could become even more devastating and detrimental. After the death of his wife, the protagonist “was always on edge. Too many conversations turned into arguments, then into fights. When [his] eyes saw red, when hot diesel coursed through these veins, nothing could stop me” (34). He turns towards violence and releases his inner frustration to other people, losing sovereignty over his actions and leading to further consequences and downfall. This demonstrates that grief can lead to unconscious and illogical actions, such as violence and addictions, that can be harmful to oneself and others around. Furthermore, in the morning, the protagonist feels like his “bed is quicksand, or one of them finger traps. Try to pull [himself] out and [he will] just get more stuck. No use. Better off watching the light play on the wall” (32). He lost motivation and drive to get up and live, and “while [he] lie here, on this bed, it's like a lifeboat lost at sea” (34), and he thought to himself, "Lifeboats are for those trying to save themselves, their lives. Am I worth saving?” (34). Overwhelmed physically and mentally, he loses his will to live, potentially leading to destructive behaviours like suicide or violence. In such circumstances, we must seek help and guidance from those we love or trust. In The Marrow Thieves, the protagonist, French, has to overcome numerous challenges, including the passing of two of his family members: Minerva and Riri. He was able to overcome the grief and remain strong because of his family and community – the support and companionship he gets, even if everyone else is suffering from the same loss. This gives him strength and resilience to continue fighting—two critical aspects of conquering grief. The protagonist in Orange may feel lonely and desperate, but there are always people he can rely on for help and companionship, such as Devon, someone who is closely connected to the protagonist. One should never have to overcome grief alone, and this text uses a gloomy and pessimistic tone to convey the negative effects of grief and the importance of seeking help. This text connects with The Marrow Thieves and Hunting By Stars because all three explore the topic of grief, family, and loss of loved ones.
